# Multifamily NW

Multifamily NW is a trade association representing rental housing providers, property managers, owners, and industry vendors in Oregon and Southwest Washington. Founded in 1992 as the [Metro](/organizations/metro) Multifamily Housing Association (MMHA), the organization rebranded to Multifamily NW in 2013 to reflect its expanded geographic reach.[^1] It is headquartered in the [Portland](/cities/portland) area.

The association represents nearly 300,000 rental homes across Oregon and is the largest apartment association in the Pacific Northwest.[^2] Its members range from small independent landlords to large property management companies operating throughout the Willamette Valley, Central Oregon, and Southern Oregon. Multifamily NW provides legislative advocacy, education, professional development, and standardized rental forms widely used across the state.

## Policy Positions

- **Rent control**: Opposes rent control measures, arguing they reduce housing supply and discourage investment in new rental housing. Actively opposed [SB 608](/legislation/2019-regular-session/sb-608) (2019) and [SB 611](/legislation/2023-regular-session/sb-611) (2023), contending that rent caps worsen the housing crisis by driving housing providers out of the market.[^3]
- **Housing supply**: Supports policies that accelerate housing production, including streamlined permitting and tax exemptions for multifamily and affordable housing development. Backed [SB 974](/legislation/2025-regular-session/sb-974) (2025) to accelerate permitting timelines and [HB 2074](/legislation/2025-regular-session/hb-2074) (2025) to extend tax exemptions for multi-unit housing projects.[^4]
- **Tenant screening**: Opposes restrictions on tenant screening criteria. Filed a lawsuit against the City of Portland in 2020 challenging the Fair Access in Renting (FAIR) ordinance, which limited landlords' ability to screen tenants based on criminal history, credit history, and rental history.[^5]
- **Regulatory burden**: Opposes mandatory inclusionary zoning, mandatory cooling requirements, and eviction bans, arguing these regulations increase costs for housing providers and reduce the availability of rental housing.[^4]
- **Renter education**: Supports funding for renter education programs, including advocating for $1.5 million in state funding for in-person Rent Well classes to educate first-time renters on financial responsibility and tenant rights.[^4]

## Key Legislative Involvement

- **[SB 1523](/legislation/2026-regular-session/sb-1523) (2026)**: Testified on legislation related to rental housing regulation during the 2026 session, continuing the organization's active engagement with the legislature on housing provider issues.
- **SB 974 (2025)**: Testified in support of permitting reform legislation aimed at accelerating housing development timelines, with Zach Lindahl representing the organization in multiple hearings.[^4]
- **HB 2074 (2025)**: Testified in support of extending tax exemptions for multi-unit and affordable housing development projects through 2032, with Jonathan Clay representing the organization.[^6]
- **SB 611 (2023)**: Testified in opposition to lowering Oregon's rent increase cap from 7% plus CPI to 3% plus CPI. Executive Director Deborah Imse argued the tighter cap would discourage housing investment and exacerbate the supply shortage.[^3]
- **[HB 2889](/legislation/2023-regular-session/hb-2889) (2023)**: Provided testimony on the Oregon Housing Needs Analysis reform legislation, which amended land use requirements for local governments related to urbanization and housing planning.[^7]
- **[SB 291](/legislation/2021-regular-session/sb-291) (2021)**: Opposed legislation requiring landlords to adopt written screening criteria and limiting the criminal history landlords could consider when evaluating applicants, with Michael Havlik testifying on behalf of the organization.[^8]
- **SB 608 (2019)**: Opposed Oregon's first-in-the-nation statewide rent control law, which capped rent increases at 7% plus CPI and restricted no-cause evictions. The organization argued that rent control reduces housing supply and hurts renters in the long run.[^9]
