# Oregon Law Center

Oregon Law Center (OLC) is a nonprofit legal aid organization headquartered in [Portland](/cities/portland), Oregon, that provides free civil legal services to low-income Oregonians. Its mission is to achieve justice for the low-income communities of Oregon by providing a full range of high-quality civil legal services.[^1] OLC operates client service offices in 11 communities across the state, serving all 36 Oregon counties as part of a coordinated statewide legal aid system alongside Legal Aid Services of Oregon and the Center for Nonprofit Legal Services.[^2]

Housing is one of OLC's primary practice areas. The organization provides legal assistance on fair housing, government-subsidized housing, landlord-tenant law, and issues related to homelessness.[^1] OLC is an active participant in Oregon legislative debates on tenant protections, rent stabilization, eviction procedures, and housing affordability. In early 2021, OLC launched its Eviction Defense Project, which provides free legal representation to low-income tenants facing eviction across the state and has assisted close to 10,000 households since its inception.[^3]

## Policy Positions

- **Rent stabilization**: Supports caps on annual rent increases to prevent housing instability for low-income tenants. Advocated for [SB 608](/legislation/2019/sb-608) (2019), Oregon's first statewide rent control law, and later supported [SB 611](/legislation/2023/sb-611) (2023) to tighten the rent increase cap formula after arguing that the original formula resulted in destabilizing 14.6% increases.[^4]
- **Eviction protections**: Supports for-cause eviction standards and expanded notice periods, arguing that no-cause evictions disproportionately harm communities of color, single women with children, seniors, and people with disabilities.[^5]
- **Tenant screening reform**: Supports restrictions on landlord use of criminal history in tenant screening and limits on applicant screening charges, testifying in favor of [SB 291](/legislation/2021/sb-291) (2021) and [HB 2967](/legislation/2025/hb-2967) (2025).[^6]
- **Eviction defense funding**: Advocates for state and local funding of legal representation for tenants in eviction proceedings, emphasizing that legal counsel significantly reduces displacement of low-income renters.[^3]
- **Emergency shelter and homelessness protections**: Supports legislation requiring local governments to allow emergency shelter siting and establishing reasonableness standards for regulation of people experiencing homelessness on public property.[^7]
- **Tenant portal protections**: Supports requirements that landlords provide alternatives to electronic tenant portals and prohibitions on mandatory digital-only rent payment, citing cases where technological failures nearly caused tenants to lose their housing.[^8]

## Key Legislative Involvement

- **[SB 1523](/legislation/2026/sb-1523) (2026)**: Testified in support of legislation requiring landlords to provide alternatives to tenant portals for rent payment and common area access, submitting multiple rounds of testimony.[^8]
- **[HB 2138](/legislation/2025/hb-2138) (2025)**: Testified on this major middle housing expansion bill, which expanded allowable middle housing types and reformed expedited land division provisions.[^9]
- **[SB 722](/legislation/2025/sb-722) (2025)**: Testified on legislation to prohibit landlords from using algorithmic software to set rents and to reduce the new-construction rent cap exemption from 15 to 7 years.[^10]
- **SB 611 (2023)**: Testified in support of tightening the annual rent increase cap for residential tenancies, with both Sybil Hebb and Becky Straus providing testimony across multiple hearings.[^4]
- **SB 291 (2021)**: Testified in support of tenant screening reform legislation requiring landlords to adopt written screening criteria and limiting consideration of criminal history in housing applications.[^6]
- **[SB 278](/legislation/2021/sb-278) (2021)**: Testified in support of legislation requiring landlords and courts to delay eviction proceedings when tenants have applied for rental assistance during the COVID-19 emergency period.[^11]
- **[HB 2009](/legislation/2021/hb-2009) (2021)**: Testified in support of temporary limitations on foreclosure remedies to protect homeowners and renters during the COVID-19 pandemic.[^12]
- **[HB 2006](/legislation/2021/hb-2006) (2021)**: Testified in support of emergency shelter siting requirements and expanded transitional housing accommodations.[^7]
- **[HB 3115](/legislation/2021/hb-3115) (2021)**: Testified in support of establishing reasonableness standards for local regulation of people experiencing homelessness on public property.[^13]
