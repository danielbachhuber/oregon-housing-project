# Oregon Farm Bureau

Oregon Farm Bureau (OFB) is a grassroots, nonpartisan, nonprofit agricultural advocacy organization headquartered in Salem, Oregon. Founded at the county level in 1919 and organized at the state level in 1932, it is Oregon's largest general agriculture advocacy organization, representing nearly 7,000 member families across all farm sizes, production methods, and commodities.[^1]

OFB's mission centers on the survival, success, and sustainability of family farms and ranches in Oregon. With 32 active County Farm Bureaus covering all 36 Oregon counties, the organization provides a unified voice for farmers and ranchers in legislative, regulatory, and legal arenas.[^1] In the housing policy space, OFB is primarily focused on protecting agricultural land from conversion to residential and industrial development, while also supporting farmworker housing improvements and rural housing flexibility.

## Policy Positions

- **Farmland protection**: Opposes legislation that would streamline or bypass Oregon's land use planning process to bring agricultural land into urban growth boundaries, viewing such measures as threats to the state's agricultural economy and food production capacity.[^2]
- **Urban growth boundary expansion**: Consistently opposes bills that would allow cities to expand UGBs onto farmland without following established land use planning procedures, including proposals for semiconductor manufacturing sites and manufactured dwelling parks.[^3][^4]
- **Farmworker housing**: Supports reforms to farmworker housing regulations, particularly efforts to distinguish permanent employer-provided housing from temporary farmworker camps under Oregon OSHA rules, arguing that overly strict camp regulations discourage farmers from providing housing to employees.[^5]
- **Rural housing flexibility**: Supports expanding accessory dwelling unit allowances on farmland and rural residential development options for low-density counties, while opposing measures that would broadly rezone agricultural land.[^6]
- **Middle housing and land use reform**: Has engaged with middle housing legislation, supporting HB 2001 (2023) which established the Oregon Housing Needs Analysis and required cities to plan for housing needs, while opposing [HB 2138](/legislation/2025/hb-2138) (2025) which expanded middle housing requirements to include urban unincorporated lands.[^7][^8]
- **Dwelling restoration**: Supports streamlined processes for restoring or replacing dwellings on rural land that were damaged or destroyed by involuntary causes such as wildfires.[^9]

## Key Legislative Involvement

- **[SB 1522](/legislation/2026/sb-1522) (2026)**: Testified in opposition to changes in urban growth boundary planning that would have allowed cities to skip established land use review and directly develop undeveloped farm and forest land.[^4]
- **SB 1564 (2026)**: Opposed adding specified lands to the City of [Woodburn](/cities/woodburn)'s urban growth boundary.[^10]
- **[HB 4082](/legislation/2026/hb-4082) (2026)**: Opposed legislation allowing cities to add sites to their UGB for manufactured dwelling parks or senior housing, reflecting concerns about farmland conversion.[^11]
- **[HB 4035](/legislation/2026/hb-4035) (2026)**: Opposed expanding eligibility for cities to amend their urban growth boundaries under a temporary program.[^12]
- **[SB 1561](/legislation/2026/sb-1561) (2026)**: Supported requiring local governments to approve restoration or replacement of dwellings rendered uninhabitable, streamlining a process important for rural property owners after disasters.[^9]
- **HB 2138 (2025)**: Opposed the expansion of middle housing requirements to urban unincorporated lands.[^8]
- **[SB 999](/legislation/2025/sb-999) (2025)**: Supported modifying the definition of "farmworker camp" to distinguish permanent employer-provided housing from temporary seasonal camps, arguing that applying temporary camp standards to permanent dwellings discouraged farmers from providing housing benefits to employees.[^5]
- **HB 2316 (2025)**: Opposed the designation of state-owned and locally nominated lands within UGBs as "home start lands" for affordable housing development.[^13]
- **[SB 4](/legislation/2023/sb-4) (2023)**: Opposed provisions allowing the Governor to designate farmland for inclusion within urban growth boundaries to support semiconductor manufacturing under the federal CHIPS Act.[^3]
- **HB 2001 (2023)**: Supported the omnibus housing bill that established the Oregon Housing Needs Analysis and required cities to plan for housing needs.[^7]
